Let's Do That Hockey

This site tells the story of professional hockey over the years 1909 to 2011 using data from Kaggle's Professional Hockey Database, a collection of historical statistics from men's professional hockey teams in North America. The dataset also includes information about each team to take the ice, each coach behind the bench, and each award to be offered, but we decided to focus on the player-level data and ask questions about the history of scoring in professional hockey.

Specifically, our goal was to look at how scoring rates - by which we mean to say how many goals, assists, and points (points = goals + assists) have been tallied each year - have changed over time, what the scoring profiles of those considered to be the best to ever play look like, and what areas of the world produce the most prolific scorers.

The first chart is a timeline describing the changes in both scoring totals and per-game rates throughout the history of professional hockey, as well as links to the events that incited those changes.

The second section features profiles of players widely thought to be the best to ever play hockey. Explore their key stats, investigate their scoring rates season-by-season, and watch their highlights.

The final section explores areas of the world that have produced professional players and how many points those players have produced.

Authors: Owen Bezick & Daniel Cowan

Conclusion

So what did we learn?

Scoring in the NHL, in terms of season totals, was largely driven by expansion. As teams joined the league, more goals and assists were tallied as there were many more players to do so. Lockouts also affected the totals, as disputes between players and the league shortened and even cancelled seasons.

In terms of per-game scoring, rule changes were key. When the center red line was introduced in 1943, as the article linked noted, breakaways became a common method of scoring, and overall scoring soared across the league. This rule change is considered to be the beginning of the modern era in professional hockey. Teams then learned how to defend these plays, and scoring slowed in years after.

The best players in hockey's history not only scored prolifically in terms of both totals and rates. They were able to string together several consecutive seasons of high-rate scoring that earned them awards, championships, and household recognition. The best goalies did the opposite; they prevented forwards from scoring an incredible percentage of the time and did so on a consistent basis.

There are still many countries who still have the opportunite to produce their first professional hockey player. Among those that have, though, Canada is clearly the king, with the USA on its tail. Scandinavia and Russia have also produced many prolific scorers.

Ontario and Quebec lead the way in terms of producing professional hockey players that score at a high rate; Minnesota and Michigan are the only US states to even come remotely close. Nunavut is the only Canadian territory yet to produce a player, while several US states, most in the Southeast, have not seen their first.
